How to Compute SVD

Here are the steps one can follow to easily compute the singular value decomposition of an arbitrary matrix

SVD's definition

Recall that Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) is a factorization of a real or complex matrix. It essentially decomposes a m $\times$ n matrix M into three matrics: $U$, $\Sigma$, and $V$, such that $M = U \Sigma V$, where $U$ is a $m \times m$ real or complex unitary matrix $(UU^T = I)$, $\Sigma$ is an $n \times n$ diagonal matrix, whose diagonal entries $\sigma_i$ are known as the singular value of the matrix M, and V is a $n \times n$ unitary matrix $(NN^T = I)$.

The Singular Values of a Matrix

Our goal is to decompose a m by n matrix $M$ into the product of three matrics, namely $U \Sigma V$. We first need to introduce the notion of singular values. A matrix's singular values is quite similar to the eigenvalues of a matrix, except that not every single matrices have eigenvalues and eigenvectors. However, every single matrics have its own singular values (proof ommited). Furthermore, we can also prove that every single matrix has a unique singular value decomposition. This means that for every abritrary matrix, we are able to compute its singular value decomposition.

By definition, the singular values of an arbitrary matrix A are the absolute values of the eigenvalues of $AA^T$, which is guaranteed to be a normal matrix. We know that $\Sigma$ is a diagonal matrix, whose diagonal entries $\sigma_i$ are the singular values of the matrix A. Once we obtained $\sigma_i$, we can easily construct $\Sigma$ based on that.

Computing U and V

U and V can be easily obtained once we have constructed $\Sigma$. U is the left-singular vectors of A, which is the set of orthonormal eigenvectors of $AA^T$. In contrast, V is the right-singular vectors of A, which is the set of orthonormal eigenvectors of $M^TM$. If you are familiar with the process of obtaining eigenvector and eigenvalues, this should not be very difficult to do.
